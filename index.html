How was phosphogypsum produced?

Phosphate rock + Sulfuric acid + Water → Phosphoric acid + Phosphogypsum + Hydrogen fluoride

Between 1968 and 2010, in Huelva, this chemical reaction was used in five factories to produce phosphoric acid.

About 2.5 million tonnes of phosphogypsum were produced annually

About 100 million tonnes of phosphogypsum are stored in nearby stacks

What substances does phosphogypsum contain?

Phosphogypsum is more than 95% constituted by gypsum, but it also contains a high concentration of both radium-226 and thorium-230 radionuclides. This property makes phosphogypsum considered a Naturally Ocurring Radioactive Material, NORM, in the European Union. It also contains approximately 20% of the uranium and more than 95% of the lead and polonium present in the original phosphate rock. Lastly, phosphogypsum also includes the remaining phosphoric acid trapped between its particles, which explains its high acidity (pH ≈ 2).

Phosphogypsum management systems

Release into the sea

Phosphogypsum, mixed with water, forms gypsum slurry. This gypsum slurry, generally untreated, is dumped into seas or rivers. This management system is implemented in places like Gabès, Tunisia, where there has been devastating consequences on marine and terrestrial fauna and flora, and also in the health of the local coastal populations. Since 1989, this system has been forbidden in the United States, as it is required to store phosphogypsum in land stacks.

Storage in controlled deposits

Phosphogypsum is stacked in large land stacks. These stacks occupy a significant and ever-growing land area, and they require costly lifetime management in both their operational and post-operational states. This system requires experience and good environmental standards to protect ground water. As an example, the first generation of phosphoric acid industries in China lacked these features, causing significant groundwater pollution.

Case of Huelva

Until 1998, about 20% of the generated phosphogypsum was discharged into the Odiel River channel, while the remaining 80% was pumped in suspension with seawater into the stacks, where phosphogypsum was decanted, and the polluted acidic (pH ≈ 2) seawater (around 107 m3 per year) was released into the Tinto River channel without any treatment. Due to a change in the environmental policy according to the OSPAR convention, from 1998 until the end of the production (31 December 2010), all the phosphogypsum was pumped with freshwater in a closed circuit and placed into the piles.

Prayon business

Description

Prayon is a phosphate-based products company headquartered in Engis, Belgium. Since its foundation, Prayon has always applied circular economy principles to its phosphogypsum production.

Central-Prayon Process

Prayon and Central Glass of Japan joined forces and developed Central-Prayon Process, consisting of the following stages:

  1. Parameters are optimized to produce high-quality phosphoric acid.
  2. Parameters are selected to produce high-quality "Alpha" Hemi-hydrate gypsum (α-HH).

These stages reduce phosphorus pentoxide concentration from about 35% to less than 0.5%. At the end, gypsum in the hemihydrate form (α-HH) is produced. A curing stage converts this hemihydrate gypsum (α-HH) into dihydrate gypsum (DH).

Dihydrate gypsum (DH) is sold to cement companies and fertiliser companies.
